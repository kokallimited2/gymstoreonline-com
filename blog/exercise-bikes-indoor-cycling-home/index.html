The Ultimate Guide to Exercise Bikes & Indoor Cycling at Home

July 2026 · GymStore Online

Exercise bikes are one of the most popular pieces of home cardio equipment — and for good reason. They deliver an effective low-impact workout that torches calories, strengthens your heart and lungs, and builds lower-body muscle endurance, all while being gentle on your joints. Unlike running, which can be hard on knees and hips, cycling provides a smooth, joint-friendly training stimulus that suits everyone from complete beginners to elite athletes.

Why Choose an Exercise Bike for Your Home Gym?

Indoor cycling offers unique advantages that make it a standout choice for home fitness. First, it is exceptionally safe — there is no risk of falling, no heavy weights to bail out of, and the motion is guided and predictable. Second, it is quiet. Modern magnetic resistance bikes operate at near-silent levels, so you can watch TV, join a video call, or workout while others are asleep. Third, it is measurable. Power meters, heart rate monitors, and cadence sensors give you precise data to track your fitness progress over time — invaluable for anyone who likes to train with structure and purpose.

A quality exercise bike like the Schwinn IC4 Indoor Cycling Bike combines magnetic resistance, Bluetooth connectivity for apps like Zwift and Peloton, and a sturdy frame at a fraction of the cost of the premium brands. Prices for a solid home exercise bike range from £200–£300 for a basic upright model to £600–£1,500 for a feature-packed smart bike.

The Three Main Types of Exercise Bikes

Understanding the differences between bike types is the first step to finding the right one for your home gym. Each design serves a slightly different purpose and suits different fitness goals.

Upright Exercise Bikes

Upright bikes mimic the position of a traditional road bike. Your hips are positioned above the pedals, and you lean slightly forward to reach the handlebars. This engages your core stabiliser muscles and provides the most efficient transfer of power to the pedals. Upright bikes are excellent for calorie burn, cardiovascular conditioning, and building leg strength. They also tend to be the most compact and affordable option. A JLL IC200 Pro Exercise Bike is a popular choice that offers magnetic resistance, a smooth belt drive, and a compact footprint perfect for home use.

Recumbent Exercise Bikes

Recumbent bikes feature a larger, chair-like seat with a backrest and place the pedals in front of you. This position distributes your weight across a larger surface area, reducing pressure on your wrists, shoulders, and lower back. Recumbent bikes are ideal for older adults, people recovering from injury, or anyone with chronic back pain who still wants a great cardio workout. The trade-off is that they take up more floor space and engage your core less than upright models. They are, however, exceptionally comfortable for longer steady-state sessions of 45–90 minutes.

Spin Bikes / Indoor Cycling Bikes

Spin bikes, also called indoor cycling bikes, are built to replicate the feel of an outdoor road bike as closely as possible. They use a heavy flywheel (usually 18–22 kg) driven by a chain or belt, creating realistic momentum and road feel. Most spin bikes use felt pad or magnetic resistance and have a freewheel mechanism that lets you coast when you stop pedalling. These are the bikes used in studio cycling classes and are perfect for high-intensity interval training (HIIT), simulated climbs, and sprint workouts. The Echelon Connect Sport Spin Bike is a solid mid-range option that works with the Echelon Fit app and offers a genuine studio cycling experience at home.

Key Features to Consider When Buying an Exercise Bike

  • Resistance Type: Magnetic resistance is quieter and requires less maintenance than felt pad or wool pad resistance. It also tends to provide smoother transitions between resistance levels. For most home users, magnetic resistance is the clear winner.
  • Flywheel Weight: Heavier flywheels (10–22 kg) provide smoother pedalling and more realistic road feel. Lighter flywheels can feel jerky, especially at lower cadences. Aim for at least 8 kg for a smooth ride.
  • Adjustability: Look for a bike with adjustable seat height, seat fore/aft position, and handlebar height (for upright/spin bikes). Proper fit is essential for comfort and injury prevention.
  • Connectivity: Many modern bikes offer Bluetooth or ANT+ connectivity to pair with training apps like Zwift, Peloton, TrainerRoad, and Rouvy. If you enjoy structured workouts or virtual riding, this feature is a game-changer.
  • Weight Capacity: Check the maximum user weight. Most quality bikes support 120–150 kg. If you are on the heavier side, look for a steel-framed model with a higher capacity.
  • Pedal System: Some bikes come with dual-purpose pedals (flat on one side, SPD cleat on the other). This is a nice feature if you plan to use cycling shoes. Otherwise, standard pedals with toe cages work fine.
  • Noise Level: Belt-driven magnetic resistance bikes are the quietest. Chain-driven models are louder and require periodic lubrication. For apartment living, go with belt-driven.

Exercise Bike vs Other Home Cardio Equipment

How do exercise bikes stack up against other popular home cardio machines? Here is a quick comparison to help you decide:

  • Exercise Bikes vs Treadmills: As covered in our treadmill buying guide, treadmills burn more calories per minute on average but are higher impact. Bikes are gentler on joints and safer for unsupervised training. Many home gyms benefit from having both — a treadmill for walking and running, and a bike for recovery days and high-intensity intervals.
  • Exercise Bikes vs Rowing Machines: Rowing machines engage your entire body — legs, core, back, and arms — making them more efficient for full-body conditioning. However, they require more technique to use effectively and can aggravate lower back issues if form breaks down. Bikes require almost no learning curve and are safer for high-intensity work.
  • Exercise Bikes vs Ellipticals: Ellipticals also provide low-impact cardio but take up significantly more floor space. Bikes are more compact, generally cheaper, and offer a more intense lower-body stimulus. Ellipticals provide some upper-body engagement through moving handlebars that bikes lack.

Sample Indoor Cycling Workout Plans

Here are three workout programmes designed for different fitness levels and time constraints. All of them can be done on any type of exercise bike.

Beginner: 20-Minute Endurance Ride

This workout builds your aerobic base and gets you comfortable on the bike. Maintain a cadence of 70–80 RPM throughout.

  • 0–5 min: Easy warm-up at resistance level 2–3
  • 5–10 min: Moderate pace at resistance level 4–5
  • 10–15 min: Steady effort at resistance level 5–6, focus on smooth pedalling
  • 15–18 min: Increase to resistance level 6–7, maintain cadence
  • 18–20 min: Cool down at resistance level 2–3

Intermediate: 30-Minute HIIT Interval Workout

Alternate between high-intensity efforts and active recovery. Increase resistance as you get fitter.

  • 0–5 min: Warm-up at easy pace
  • 5–7 min: Sprint at 90+ RPM, resistance 5–6
  • 7–9 min: Active recovery at 60–70 RPM, resistance 3
  • 9–11 min: Sprint at 90+ RPM, resistance 6–7
  • 11–13 min: Active recovery
  • 13–15 min: Sprint at 95+ RPM, resistance 5–6
  • 15–17 min: Active recovery
  • 17–19 min: Sprint at 90+ RPM, resistance 7–8
  • 19–21 min: Active recovery
  • 21–23 min: Sprint at 95+ RPM, resistance 6–7
  • 23–25 min: Active recovery
  • 25–30 min: Cool down at easy pace

Advanced: 45-Minute Climbing Workout

Simulate hill climbs with sustained high resistance, standing out of the saddle on the toughest sections.

  • 0–5 min: Warm-up, gradually increase resistance
  • 5–12 min: Seated climb, resistance 7–8, cadence 60–65 RPM
  • 12–15 min: Standing climb, resistance 8–9, cadence 55–60 RPM
  • 15–22 min: Seated moderate pace, resistance 5–6, cadence 75–80 RPM
  • 22–29 min: Seated climb, resistance 8–9, cadence 60–65 RPM
  • 29–32 min: Standing sprint, resistance 6–7, cadence 85+ RPM
  • 32–39 min: Seated steady-state, resistance 6–7, cadence 70–75 RPM
  • 39–42 min: Final push — standing climb at maximum manageable resistance, 55–60 RPM
  • 42–45 min: Cool down, decreasing resistance gradually

Tips for Getting the Most Out of Your Exercise Bike

  • Set up your bike correctly. Your knee should be slightly bent (25–35 degrees) at the bottom of the pedal stroke. Your handlebars should be at or slightly above seat height for comfort. A poorly fitted bike leads to knee pain and reduced performance.
  • Use a heart rate monitor. Target zones: Zone 2 (60–70% max HR) for endurance rides, Zone 4 (80–90% max HR) for intervals. Most bikes with Bluetooth connectivity can pair with a chest strap or armband monitor.
  • Stand up occasionally. Standing out of the saddle shifts load to different muscle groups, increases calorie burn, and relieves pressure on your sit bones. Aim to stand for 30–60 seconds every 10 minutes during steady rides.
  • Vary your training. Alternate between long steady rides, HIIT sessions, and climbing workouts. This prevents boredom and ensures balanced cardiovascular development. Combine bike training with our strength training guide for a complete home fitness programme.
  • Protect your floor. Even though bikes are stationary, the pressure points can dent wooden floors or damage carpet. Place a heavy-duty gym mat underneath to protect your flooring and reduce vibration.
  • Stay hydrated. Keep a water bottle within reach. You can lose 0.5–1 litre of fluid per hour during intense indoor cycling due to sweat, even if you do not feel as hot as you would during outdoor exercise.

Maintenance and Longevity

Exercise bikes are among the most low-maintenance pieces of home gym equipment. Here is what you need to do to keep yours running smoothly for years:

  • Clean the frame weekly. Wipe down the frame, seat, and handlebars with a damp cloth after use. Sweat is corrosive and can damage paint and chrome over time.
  • Check bolts monthly. Pedals, seat posts, and handlebar stems can work loose over time. Give them a quick check and tighten as needed.
  • Lubricate the chain (if applicable). Chain-driven bikes need chain lubrication every 3–6 months. Belt-driven bikes are maintenance-free — yet another reason to choose magnetic belt-drive if you are buying new.
  • Tension the belt. Belt-driven bikes may need occasional belt tension adjustment as the belt stretches. This is usually a simple bolt adjustment on the rear of the frame.
  • Store in a dry place. Humidity accelerates rust and corrosion. If your bike lives in a garage or shed, consider using a dehumidifier or bike cover.

Final Thoughts

An exercise bike is one of the smartest investments you can make for your home gym. It delivers reliable, measurable, low-impact cardio that complements every other form of training you do. Whether you choose a compact upright for gentle daily rides, a recumbent for injury-friendly long sessions, or a spin bike for heart-pumping HIIT workouts, the key is consistency — and an exercise bike makes it almost too easy to get your daily movement in, regardless of weather, time of day, or how busy your schedule is.
