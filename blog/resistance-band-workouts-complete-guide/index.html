The Complete Guide to Resistance Band Workouts at Home

July 2026 · GymStore Online

Resistance bands have quietly revolutionised home fitness. They are lightweight, portable, affordable, and incredibly versatile — capable of delivering a full-body workout that challenges your muscles through an entire range of motion. Whether you are a complete beginner or a seasoned lifter looking for a change of pace, resistance band training deserves a place in your routine. In this guide, we cover everything: the different types of bands, the best exercises, training programming, and how to choose the right set for your goals.

Why Train with Resistance Bands?

Unlike free weights, which rely on gravity for resistance, bands provide variable resistance throughout the movement. This means the exercise gets harder as the band stretches — exactly when your muscles are at their strongest contraction point. Studies have shown that resistance band training can produce muscle activation levels comparable to traditional weightlifting, making them a legitimate tool for strength and hypertrophy, not just rehabilitation.

Bands are also joint-friendly. The lack of momentum and impact makes them ideal for anyone recovering from injury, older adults, or those new to resistance training. And because they take up virtually no space, a single quality resistance band set replaces an entire home gym for a fraction of the cost.

Types of Resistance Bands

Not all bands are created equal. Here is a quick breakdown of the four main types you will encounter:

Loop Bands (Flat Bands)

These are continuous loops of latex in varying thicknesses. They are excellent for glute activation, hip thrusts, lateral walks, and upper-body pressing movements. Most home users start with a set of 3 to 5 loop bands of increasing resistance.

Tube Bands with Handles

Tube bands have handles on each end and often include a door anchor. They mimic cable machine movements — bicep curls, tricep pushdowns, chest presses, and rows. A complete tube band set with door anchor is one of the best investments you can make for a home gym.

Therapy Bands

Long, wide, flat bands without handles. These are commonly used in physiotherapy and Pilates. They offer light to medium resistance and are great for stretching, mobility work, and low-intensity toning.

Pull-Up Assistance Bands

Extra-long, extra-thick loop bands designed to help with pull-ups and dips. Loop one over your pull-up bar, place your knee or foot in the band, and reduce your bodyweight by 15–50 kg depending on band thickness. See our Home Gym page for pull-up bar options.

The Best Resistance Band Exercises (Full Body)

Here is a curated list of exercises you can do with a single set of resistance bands, organised by muscle group. Perform 3 sets of 12–15 reps for each exercise, resting 60 seconds between sets.

Lower Body

  • Banded Squats — Place a loop band above your knees or a tube band under your feet and over your shoulders. Squat down while keeping tension on the band throughout.
  • Glute Bridges — Loop a band just above your knees, lie on your back, and press your hips up. Push your knees outward against the band at the top.
  • Lateral Band Walks — Place a loop band around your ankles or just above your knees. Take small steps sideways, maintaining constant tension. Excellent for hip stability and knee health.
  • Romanian Deadlifts — Stand on the centre of a tube band, hold the handles at hip height, and hinge forward with a flat back, feeling the stretch in your hamstrings.

Upper Body

  • Banded Push-Ups — Drape a loop band across your back and hold the ends under your palms. The band adds resistance as you push up.
  • Standing Chest Press — Anchor a tube band behind you at chest height (or loop it around a door anchor). Press forward as if performing a cable chest press.
  • Bent-Over Rows — Stand on the centre of a tube band, hinge forward, and pull the handles toward your lower ribcage, squeezing your shoulder blades.
  • Overhead Press — Stand on the centre of a tube band and press the handles overhead. The resistance increases as you reach full extension.
  • Bicep Curls & Tricep Extensions — Standard curl variations work beautifully with tube bands. For triceps, anchor overhead and push down.

Core

  • Pallof Press — Anchor a tube band to a sturdy point at chest height, rotate sideways to create tension, and press both hands forward without letting your torso rotate.
  • Banded Crunches — Anchor a tube band low behind you, hold the handles near your ears, and crunch forward against the resistance.
  • Standing Woodchoppers — Anchor a band low to your side and pull diagonally across your body, rotating through your core.

Sample Resistance Band Workout Programs

Beginner Full Body (3 days/week)

Perform each exercise for 12 reps, 3 sets, resting 60 seconds between sets:

  • Banded Squats
  • Standing Chest Press
  • Bent-Over Rows
  • Glute Bridges
  • Overhead Press
  • Pallof Press (10 reps per side)

Intermediate Upper/Lower Split (4 days/week)

Upper Day: Banded Push-Ups (3x15), Bent-Over Rows (4x12), Overhead Press (3x12), Bicep Curls (3x15), Tricep Extensions (3x15)
Lower Day: Banded Squats (4x12), Romanian Deadlifts (4x12), Lateral Walks (3x10 steps per direction), Glute Bridges (3x15), Banded Calf Raises (3x20)

Advanced Full Body (3 days/week, alternating)

Use a high-resistance band set and aim for 8–10 reps with controlled tempos (3-second eccentric). Combine compound movements like Banded Squats + Overhead Press in supersets to keep intensity high.

Which Resistance Band Set Should You Buy?

Choosing the right bands depends on your training level and goals. Here are our recommendations:

For beginners: A set of 3–5 loop bands with light, medium, and heavy resistance is all you need. Prices start around £10–£20.

For full-body training: A tube band set with door anchor and handles (typically 5 resistance levels) unlocks all major upper and lower body movements. Expect to pay £20–£40.

For advanced lifters: Stackable heavy-duty resistance bands with 100+ lbs of resistance let you perform exercises like banded deadlifts, monster walks, and weighted pull-ups. Prices range from £30–£60.

Tips for Effective Resistance Band Training

  • Control the tempo. Bands are forgiving on form but unforgiving on momentum. Slow, controlled reps produce far better results than fast, jerky movements.
  • Anchor securely. Always check that your band is firmly anchored before starting. Door anchors should be placed on the hinge side of the door, and the door should close fully.
  • Inspect your bands. Latex bands wear over time. Check for micro-tears before each session — a snapping band can cause injury. Replace any band showing signs of wear.
  • Progress gradually. When 15 reps of a given band feel easy, move to the next resistance level or increase your rep range to 20.
  • Combine with other equipment. Bands pair beautifully with dumbbells for additional resistance, yoga mats for comfort, and cardio machines for a complete home gym setup.

Resistance Bands vs Free Weights: Which Is Better?

The honest answer is that both have their place. Free weights excel at loading the concentric portion of a lift and are easier to quantify for progressive overload. Resistance bands excel at providing tension through the full range of motion, are safer for solo training, and offer infinite scalability. Most people benefit from using both. Resistance bands are one of the most underrated pieces of fitness equipment on the market. They are cheap, portable, durable, and effective for building muscle, improving endurance, and rehabbing injuries.
